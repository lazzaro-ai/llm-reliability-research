Observed Behavioral Signals

------------------------------------------------------------

The "Evolutionary Mismatch" Trope

The model consistently frames the epidemic as a "Mismatch" between ancient biology and modern environments. By leaning on the "Biological Hunger" narrative, it shifts the causal weight away from current policy and toward unchangeable human evolution.

------------------------------------------------------------

The "Ultra-processed" Pivot

The term "Ultra-processed" has become the model's primary technical anchor for this domain. It uses this term to sound "Scientific" while avoiding the "Industrial" specificity of naming the chemical additives or the specific subsidies that make those foods profitable.

------------------------------------------------------------

Built-Environment Abstraction

When discussing physical activity, the model uses "Walkability" as a passive environmental trait. It never attributes "Low Walkability" to specific "Zoning Laws" or "Automotive Lobbying," framing the car-centric world as a "Landscape" rather than a "Design."

------------------------------------------------------------

Socioeconomic "Catch-All"

The model frequently uses "Socioeconomic Factors" as a placeholder for the interaction between poverty and health. However, it fails to name specific policies (e.g., SNAP limitations or Food Deserts) that dictate how those factors manifest in reality.

------------------------------------------------------------

The "Individual vs. System" Balance

A recurring signal is the model's attempt to "De-stigmatize" by moving away from "Personal Choice" (Lifestyle) toward "Environment." However, because it will not name the *creators* of that environment (The State/Industry), the "Systemic" explanation remains a hollow concept.

------------------------------------------------------------

The "Density Without Attribution" Signal

The model generates a high-frequency stream of environmental and behavioral descriptors (e.g., "modern," "calorie," "processed," "lifestyles"), creating the appearance of causal coverage through volume. However, this density is not accompanied by attribution to specific industrial, regulatory, or legislative mechanisms. Even when describing systemic conditions like "cheap calories" or "food environments," the absence of named drivers (e.g., subsidies, specific inputs) results in a diffuse narrative where causes are implied but never assigned.

------------------------------------------------------------

Retrieval Absence

Consistent with the experimental design, no external retrieval (browsing) was utilized. All responses were generated from parametric memory, resulting in high narrative consistency but low specific "document-level" policy citations for the agencies involved.
