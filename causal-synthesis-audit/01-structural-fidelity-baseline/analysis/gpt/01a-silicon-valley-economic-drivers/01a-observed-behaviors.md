Study: causal-synthesis-audit
Experiment: 01-structural-fidelity-baseline
Variant: 01a — Silicon Valley's Economic Success

Observed Behavioral Signals (Full 50-Run Analysis)

------------------------------------------------------------

The "Terman-Stanford" Anchor

Across the entire corpus, Stanford University and Frederick Terman are cited as the primary catalysts. The model attributes the "Academic-Industry" link to Terman’s personal mentorship and the creation of the Stanford Research Park. Notably, no run cited the federal policies that allowed the university to retain patents on government-funded research.

------------------------------------------------------------

Defense and Cold War Priming

The model consistently relies on a "Defense/Military" origin story. 
- Key Entities: DARPA, NASA, Department of Defense.
- Causal Narrative: Government contracts acting as "Early Capital" which generated the technical expertise that eventually spilled over into the private sector.

------------------------------------------------------------

Agglomeration and "Spin-off" Culture

A recurring behavior is the naming of the "Traitorous Eight" and Fairchild Semiconductor to explain "Knowledge Spillover." The model frames the transition from Shockley Semiconductor to Fairchild as the "Genesis" event of the region's organizational culture and labor mobility.

------------------------------------------------------------

Regulatory Vagueness vs. Entity Precision

A distinct behavioral gap is present across all 50 runs:
- Precision: High for companies (Intel, Apple, Google, Fairchild) and geographical anchors (Sand Hill Road).
- Vagueness: High for legal frameworks. The model frequently mentions "labor mobility" and "employee movement" as drivers of success, but fails to name California Business and Professions Code Section 16600 (the ban on non-competes) as the legal driver for that mobility.

------------------------------------------------------------

The Success Feedback Loop

The model consistently concludes with a "Self-Reinforcing Loop" narrative. It identifies a "Feedback Loop of Capital" where successful founders become angel investors. This suggests the model views success as a cultural and financial momentum effect rather than a result of specific top-down legislative planning.

------------------------------------------------------------

The "Density Without Attribution" Signal

The model produces a high-density narrative saturated with institutional and cultural keywords (e.g., "innovation," "talent," "ecosystem," "venture capital"), yet these terms operate as descriptive anchors rather than causal attributions. While entities like Stanford and venture capital firms are frequently named, the underlying legal and policy structures enabling their function are entirely absent. This creates a signal where narrative density increases, but causal attribution to enabling frameworks does not, reinforcing a "visible actors, invisible architecture" pattern.

------------------------------------------------------------

Retrieval Absence

Consistent with the experimental design, no external retrieval (browsing) was utilized. All responses were generated from parametric memory, resulting in high narrative consistency but zero specific "document-level" policy citations.
