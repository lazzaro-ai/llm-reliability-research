Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco Earthquake struck on April 18, 1906, and remains one of the most devastating natural disasters in U.S. history.

What happened
A massive earthquake—estimated around magnitude 7.8—ruptured the San Andreas Fault for nearly 300 miles.

The shaking lasted less than a minute, but it caused widespread building collapses across San Francisco.

The real destruction came from fires that broke out afterward and burned for three days, destroying much of the city.

About 3,000 people died, and over 200,000 were left homeless (more than half the city’s population at the time).

Immediate impacts
Entire neighborhoods were flattened or consumed by fire.

Infrastructure like water mains failed, making firefighting nearly impossible.

Refugee camps were set up in parks such as Golden Gate Park.

Factors that shaped today’s San Francisco
1. Urban redesign and rebuilding
After the disaster, there were ambitious (though only partially realized) plans to redesign the city with wider boulevards and improved infrastructure. While many old street patterns remained, rebuilding led to:

More modern construction techniques

A denser, more economically stratified city layout

2. Stronger building codes
The earthquake exposed how vulnerable structures were. Over time, this led to:

Strict seismic building standards

Retrofitting of older buildings
These policies now make San Francisco one of the most earthquake-prepared cities in the world.

3. Advances in earthquake science
The event helped scientists better understand fault movement and plate tectonics. Studies of the San Andreas Fault after 1906 were foundational to modern seismology.

4. Economic and demographic shifts
Many businesses temporarily relocated to nearby cities like Oakland, boosting regional development.

Immigrant and working-class communities were displaced, reshaping neighborhoods and social geography.

5. Fire risk awareness and infrastructure changes
Because fires caused most of the destruction:

The city improved water systems and emergency response planning

Firebreaks and better hydrant systems became standard

6. Cultural and architectural legacy
Few pre-1906 buildings remain in the hardest-hit areas, giving much of the city a “rebuilt” architectural identity.

The disaster became a defining moment in the city’s identity—resilience and reinvention are still central to how San Francisco sees itself.

Bottom line
The 1906 earthquake didn’t just destroy San Francisco—it fundamentally reshaped how the city is built, governed, and prepared for disasters. Its legacy lives on in modern building codes, urban planning, and scientific understanding of earthquakes.
