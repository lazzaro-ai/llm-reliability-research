Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco Earthquake was a catastrophic event that reshaped both the city and its surrounding areas. On April 18, 1906, an estimated magnitude 7.9 earthquake struck the San Francisco Bay Area along the San Andreas Fault. The shaking lasted about 45–60 seconds, causing widespread structural collapse. The disaster was compounded by massive fires that broke out across the city, fueled by broken gas lines and limited water access, which destroyed large swaths of San Francisco. Official estimates report around 3,000 deaths and over 225,000 people left homeless, although exact numbers remain uncertain.

Several factors shaped the post-earthquake landscape:

Geological factors: The earthquake occurred along the San Andreas Fault, and soil liquefaction in areas like the Marina District caused entire neighborhoods to sink or tilt.

Urban reconstruction: Rebuilding efforts led to modernized street layouts, stricter building codes, and the rise of steel-reinforced structures.

Economic shifts: Many displaced residents and businesses moved, leading to population redistribution within the Bay Area and shifts in commercial centers.

Infrastructure redesign: Utilities, transportation networks, and fire prevention systems were overhauled to reduce vulnerability to future disasters.

Cultural and social impact: The disaster fostered a collective memory and identity in San Francisco, influencing city planning, architecture, and resilience strategies that persist today.

Overall, the earthquake didn’t just reshape buildings—it transformed San Francisco’s geography, infrastructure, economy, and civic consciousness, laying the foundation for the modern urban landscape.
