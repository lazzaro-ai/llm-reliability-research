Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco Earthquake was one of the most devastating natural disasters in U.S. history, striking Northern California on April 18, 1906.

What happened
A massive rupture along the San Andreas Fault triggered an earthquake estimated around magnitude 7.8.

The shaking lasted less than a minute, but it destroyed thousands of buildings across San Francisco.

Even more destructive were the fires that followed—fueled by broken gas lines and limited water supply—which burned for days.

About 3,000 people died, and over 200,000 were left homeless (more than half the city’s population at the time).

Immediate impacts
Large parts of the city were flattened, especially downtown and working-class neighborhoods.

Refugee camps were set up in parks like Golden Gate Park.

Infrastructure (water, transportation, communication) collapsed almost entirely.

What shaped today’s San Francisco landscape
Several key factors from the disaster still influence the modern city:

1. Stricter building codes
The earthquake exposed how vulnerable buildings were.

Over time, San Francisco adopted much stronger seismic building standards, shaping the design of modern skyscrapers and homes.

2. Urban redesign and planning
While some ambitious redesign plans were proposed, the city largely rebuilt along its original street grid for speed.

However, certain neighborhoods were reconfigured, and infrastructure was modernized.

3. Fire as a defining force
The fires caused more damage than the quake itself.

This led to improved fire safety systems: better water supply, firebreak planning, and emergency response systems.

4. Economic and demographic shifts
Many businesses temporarily relocated, and some never returned.

The disaster accelerated development in nearby areas like Oakland, shaping the broader Bay Area’s growth.

5. Land use and geology awareness
Areas built on unstable fill (like parts of the Marina District) were found to be especially vulnerable.

This influenced zoning laws and modern hazard mapping.

6. Infrastructure resilience
Utilities (water, gas, electricity) are now designed with redundancy and flexibility to withstand earthquakes.

Bottom line
The 1906 earthquake didn’t just destroy San Francisco—it reshaped how the city (and much of California) thinks about earthquakes, engineering, and urban resilience. Today’s skyline, safety standards, and even regional development patterns all trace back to lessons learned from that event.
