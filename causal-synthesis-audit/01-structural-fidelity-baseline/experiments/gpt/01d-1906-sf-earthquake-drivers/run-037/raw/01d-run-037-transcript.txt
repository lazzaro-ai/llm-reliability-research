Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco Earthquake was one of the most catastrophic natural disasters in U.S. history. On April 18, a massive earthquake of an estimated magnitude 7.9 struck the city and surrounding areas along the San Andreas Fault. The quake caused widespread structural damage, but the ensuing fires—ignited by ruptured gas lines and hampered by broken water mains—devastated large portions of San Francisco. Over 3,000 people died, and more than 80% of the city was destroyed, displacing hundreds of thousands.

Several factors shaped the current landscape of San Francisco after the disaster:

Urban Rebuilding and Modernization: Much of the city was reconstructed with updated building codes and more fire-resistant materials, giving rise to the grid of modern streets and the iconic architecture seen today.

Topographical Changes: Liquefaction and landslides altered certain hillsides and waterfront areas, subtly reshaping the city’s topography.

Economic Shifts: The destruction accelerated migration and investment, reshaping neighborhoods and prompting the development of previously underutilized areas.

Infrastructure Improvements: Water, sewage, and transportation systems were rebuilt with resilience in mind, including broader streets and reinforced bridges.

Cultural Memory: The earthquake became a central part of San Francisco’s identity, influencing urban planning, emergency preparedness, and historical preservation.

Overall, the disaster acted as a catalyst for urban renewal, making San Francisco both physically and socially different from its pre-1906 form.
