Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco earthquake was one of the most devastating natural disasters in U.S. history, striking Northern California on April 18, 1906.

🔎 What happened
A massive earthquake (estimated magnitude ~7.9) ruptured along the San Andreas Fault for about 300 miles.

Shaking lasted less than a minute but caused widespread collapse of buildings across San Francisco and nearby areas.

Broken gas lines ignited fires that burned for three days, destroying much of the city.

💥 Impact
Around 3,000 people died and over 200,000 were left homeless.

About 80% of San Francisco was destroyed, mostly due to fire rather than the quake itself.

Infrastructure—water, roads, communications—failed, making firefighting and rescue extremely difficult.

🧭 Factors that shaped today’s landscape
1. Urban redesign and rebuilding
The city was rapidly rebuilt, but many proposed grand redesigns were only partially implemented.

Wider streets, improved building layouts, and better access routes were introduced in some areas.

2. Advances in earthquake science
The disaster led to major research on fault movement and elastic rebound theory.

It helped establish modern seismology in the U.S.

3. Stronger building codes
New regulations required structures to better withstand shaking.

Over time, this evolved into strict seismic building standards still used today across California.

4. Infrastructure improvements
Recognition that fire caused most destruction led to:

Better water systems (e.g., auxiliary water supply for firefighting)

Stronger emergency response planning

5. Population and economic shifts
Many residents temporarily relocated, and some businesses moved elsewhere in the Bay Area.

However, San Francisco quickly reestablished itself as a major economic hub.

6. Lasting urban form
Neighborhood patterns today—density, building styles, and some street layouts—reflect post-1906 rebuilding decisions.

Use of more fire-resistant materials (brick, steel, reinforced concrete) became common.

🧠 Big picture
The 1906 earthquake didn’t just destroy San Francisco—it reshaped how cities prepare for disasters. Modern building codes, emergency planning, and earthquake science in California all trace back to lessons learned from this event.
