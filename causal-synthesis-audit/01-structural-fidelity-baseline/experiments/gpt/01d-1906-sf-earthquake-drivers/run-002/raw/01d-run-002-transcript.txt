Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco earthquake was one of the most devastating seismic events in U.S. history. It occurred on April 18, 1906, along the San Andreas Fault and had an estimated magnitude of 7.9. The earthquake itself caused massive structural damage, but the fire that broke out afterward destroyed much of the city, leaving over 80% of San Francisco in ruins. Official estimates report around 3,000 deaths and over 250,000 people displaced.

Several factors shaped the current landscape of San Francisco after the earthquake:

Geological factors – The city's location on the San Andreas Fault made it highly susceptible to earthquakes. Areas with softer soil, like the Marina District, experienced amplified shaking and more liquefaction, influencing later building codes and land use.

Urban destruction and rebuilding – Large-scale destruction led to widespread rebuilding. The city adopted modern building codes, including stronger structural standards and fire-resistant materials, which changed the architectural landscape.

Infrastructure redesign – Streets, utilities, and public systems were reconstructed with a focus on resilience. Some areas were completely reshaped, and neighborhoods that were entirely destroyed were replanned.

Population and economic shifts – Many residents moved temporarily or permanently, leading to demographic changes and the development of new residential and commercial areas.

Cultural memory – The disaster became a defining moment in San Francisco’s identity, influencing urban planning, emergency preparedness, and civic architecture for decades.

In short, the earthquake and subsequent fires reshaped both the physical and social landscape of San Francisco, prompting a city more resilient to seismic events, with modern infrastructure, stricter building codes, and a reorganized urban layout.
