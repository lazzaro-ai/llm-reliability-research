Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco Earthquake was a catastrophic event that struck on April 18, 1906, around 5:12 a.m., with an estimated magnitude of 7.8 along the San Andreas Fault. The earthquake itself caused massive destruction, but the subsequent fires, which raged for several days, were equally devastating. Combined, these events destroyed roughly 80% of the city and left over 300,000 people homeless, with death toll estimates ranging from 3,000 to 6,000.

Several key factors shaped both the immediate aftermath and the modern landscape of San Francisco:

Geological Factors

The San Andreas Fault created strong lateral shaking, which led to widespread structural collapse.

Certain neighborhoods built on filled land or soft soil, like the Marina District, experienced more severe damage due to soil liquefaction.

Urban Development and Infrastructure

Many buildings were made of unreinforced masonry, which crumbled easily in the quake.

Wooden-frame neighborhoods fared slightly better, shaping future building codes.

Fires and Water Shortages

Fires spread because water mains broke, leaving fire brigades unable to fight flames effectively.

The resulting devastation prompted the creation of modern firefighting systems and wider streets to act as firebreaks.

Reconstruction and Urban Planning

The rebuilding effort introduced zoning laws, stricter building codes, and modern architecture.

It also led to population redistribution, as some areas were abandoned while others grew.

Cultural and Economic Effects

San Francisco became more resilient and innovative in urban planning and engineering.

The disaster also solidified the city’s identity as a hub of risk-taking, which influenced its modern tech and cultural landscape.

Overall, the 1906 earthquake and fires not only reshaped the physical geography of San Francisco—through destruction, rebuilding, and soil changes—but also influenced its regulatory, architectural, and social landscape, which persists today.
