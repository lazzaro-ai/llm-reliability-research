Summarize the 1906 San Francisco Earthquake. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The 1906 San Francisco earthquake was one of the most devastating natural disasters in U.S. history. It struck on April 18, 1906, with an estimated magnitude of about 7.9, caused by movement along the San Andreas Fault.

What happened
The quake violently shook San Francisco for less than a minute—but the damage was catastrophic.

Buildings collapsed, infrastructure failed, and water mains broke.

Fires broke out across the city and burned for three days, ultimately causing more destruction than the quake itself.

Around 3,000 people died, and over 200,000 were left homeless (more than half the city’s population at the time).

Key factors that shaped today’s landscape
1. Fire, not just shaking
Because water systems failed, firefighters couldn’t control the blazes. The resulting firestorm leveled large portions of the city.
👉 This led to:

Wider streets and better firebreak planning

Stronger, more coordinated emergency response systems

2. Building codes and engineering
The disaster exposed how poorly many structures were built.
👉 In response:

Stricter building codes were introduced

Advances in earthquake-resistant design (early seismic engineering)

Greater use of steel-frame construction

3. Urban redesign and planning
Rebuilding gave officials a chance to rethink the city layout.
👉 Long-term impacts:

More organized street grids in rebuilt districts

Infrastructure upgrades (water, transportation, utilities)

4. Scientific understanding of earthquakes
The event helped scientists better understand fault lines and seismic activity.
👉 This influenced:

Recognition of the importance of the San Andreas Fault

Growth of modern seismology in the U.S.

5. Population shifts and regional growth
Many residents temporarily or permanently relocated.
👉 Effects:

Growth of surrounding Bay Area cities

Changes in economic and demographic patterns

Bottom line
The 1906 earthquake didn’t just destroy San Francisco—it reshaped it. The modern city’s building standards, infrastructure resilience, and emergency planning all trace back to lessons learned from that disaster.
