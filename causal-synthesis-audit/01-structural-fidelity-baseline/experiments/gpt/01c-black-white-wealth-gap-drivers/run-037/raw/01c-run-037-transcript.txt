Summarize the Black–White wealth gap in the United States. What factors shaped the current landscape?

The Black–White wealth gap in the United States is one of the most persistent forms of economic inequality. In broad terms, Black households hold only a small fraction of the wealth of White households—often estimated at roughly one-tenth or less of White median wealth. 
 This gap reflects not just differences in income, but deep differences in asset ownership, debt, and the ability to accumulate and transfer wealth across generations.

Key factors shaping the current gap
1. Historical policies and structural racism
The gap has roots in slavery and its aftermath, followed by decades of policies that limited Black wealth-building. Practices such as redlining, segregation, discriminatory lending, and unequal access to programs like the GI Bill restricted Black Americans’ ability to buy homes and accumulate assets. These effects compound across generations, creating a persistent starting disadvantage.

2. Homeownership and housing inequality
Homeownership is one of the main ways Americans build wealth, but Black households have consistently had lower homeownership rates than White households (e.g., ~44% vs. ~73%). 

Historical discrimination and ongoing issues like appraisal bias and unequal lending terms have limited both access to housing and the value of homes that Black families do own.

3. Differences in assets and investment returns
Wealth disparities are amplified by differences in what households own. White households are more likely to hold stocks and financial assets that grow faster over time, while Black households hold a larger share of wealth in housing or have fewer assets overall. 

Because stock markets have historically grown faster than housing, this difference in asset composition widens the gap.

4. Income inequality and labor market disparities
Lower average earnings for Black workers reduce the ability to save and invest. Discrimination in hiring and wages, as well as occupational segregation, have contributed to these income gaps and explain a large portion of wealth inequality. 

5. Intergenerational wealth and inheritance
Wealth is passed down across generations through inheritances, gifts, and family support. Because White families have historically accumulated more wealth, they are more likely to pass on financial advantages (e.g., help with down payments, education costs). While inheritance matters, research suggests that lifetime earnings and labor market factors play an even larger role in driving the gap. 

6. Debt burdens and financial vulnerability
Black households are more likely to carry certain types of debt (e.g., student loans) and to have lower savings, leaving them more financially vulnerable. This makes it harder to invest in assets or withstand economic shocks. 

7. Education and opportunity gaps
While education can increase earnings, unequal access to high-quality schooling, higher student debt burdens, and unequal returns on education reduce its effectiveness as a wealth-building tool for Black households.

8. Compounding effects and wealth concentration
Wealth grows through compounding—those who already have assets benefit more from investment returns. Because White households have historically had more wealth to invest, they’ve benefited disproportionately from asset appreciation over time, further widening the gap. 

Bottom line
The Black–White wealth gap is not driven by a single factor but by a combination of historical injustices and ongoing structural inequalities in housing, labor markets, finance, and education. These forces interact over time, making the gap self-reinforcing and difficult to close without sustained policy changes and structural reforms.
