The Black–White wealth gap in the United States refers to the large and persistent difference in total household wealth (assets minus debts) between Black and White households. Wealth—not just income—includes savings, home equity, stocks, business ownership, and inheritances, and it accumulates across generations. Despite some progress over time, the gap remains substantial and structurally rooted.

How large is the gap?
Median wealth for White households has historically been many times higher than for Black households. For example, one analysis shows White households held roughly about 10× the wealth of Black households in recent decades. 

In more recent estimates, the difference remains on the order of hundreds of thousands of dollars between median households. 

A notable feature is that a much higher share of Black households have zero or negative net worth compared to White households. 

The gap has narrowed at times historically (especially during parts of the mid-20th century) but has stagnated or widened in recent decades. 

Key factors that shaped the current landscape
1. Historical policies and structural exclusion
A major driver is the long legacy of policies that limited Black Americans’ access to wealth-building opportunities:

Exclusion from New Deal-era housing programs and subsidized mortgages

Unequal access to GI Bill benefits (education and homeownership)

Segregation and discriminatory practices in lending and housing markets (e.g., redlining)

These policies helped create compounding advantages for White households in homeownership, education, and asset accumulation. 

2. Intergenerational wealth and inheritance
Wealth is passed down across generations, and historical disparities have accumulated:

White families have had more opportunities to build and transfer wealth via inheritance, home equity, and financial assets

Intergenerational transfers amplify existing advantages, making it easier to invest in education, housing, and businesses

Inheritance is a meaningful but not sole driver of the gap; broader lifetime advantages matter more overall 

3. Differences in asset ownership
The composition of wealth differs significantly:

Homeownership: White households have much higher homeownership rates, and housing equity is a primary source of wealth for most Americans

Financial assets: White households are far more likely to hold stocks and retirement investments, which tend to grow faster than housing wealth

Business ownership: White households have greater access to business equity and capital, which can generate substantial wealth over time 

These differences mean that even when incomes are similar, wealth accumulates unevenly.

4. Income and labor market disparities
Income gaps contribute directly to savings and investment differences:

Lower average earnings and employment opportunities for Black workers reduce the ability to save and invest

Occupational segregation and wage gaps persist due to differences in education access, discrimination, and labor market dynamics

Over time, lower lifetime earnings translate into lower accumulated wealth 

5. Education, credit, and debt disparities
Black households, on average, carry different debt burdens, including higher student loan debt in some cases

Limited access to affordable credit and higher borrowing costs can constrain asset building

Differences in credit access affect the ability to buy homes, start businesses, or invest 

6. Housing market dynamics and shocks
Housing is the largest asset for most households, and disparities in access to homeownership have long-term effects

Events like the 2008 housing crisis disproportionately reduced Black wealth due to lower baseline asset levels and higher vulnerability

More recent housing price increases have benefited existing homeowners, reinforcing gaps 

7. Capital gains and investment returns
Wealth growth increasingly comes from financial markets (stocks, business equity), which are disproportionately held by White households

Because these assets appreciate faster than wages, households already owning them see compounding gains, widening disparities over time 

8. Systemic and cumulative effects
The modern wealth gap is best understood as the cumulative result of:

Historical discrimination

Policy design and uneven access to public programs

Differences in asset ownership and returns

Compounding intergenerational advantages

Rather than a single cause, these factors reinforce each other across decades, producing a persistent gap that changes slowly even when income gaps narrow.

Bottom line
The Black–White wealth gap is large, persistent, and primarily driven by structural and historical factors rather than any single contemporary cause. Key drivers include unequal access to asset-building opportunities (especially housing and financial markets), intergenerational wealth transfers, labor market disparities, and the long-term effects of discriminatory policies. Wealth inequality persists because wealth compounds over time, and past disadvantages continue to shape present-day outcomes.
